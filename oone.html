A Manifesto · Josh Harrison · December 2021

Home as Possibility

I write this with an acknowledgment of my privilege as both a white gay male within the Queer community and as someone that has always had a shelter over their head. I write this from the perspective of having grown up with a single mother raising two children, from having watched her make sacrificial life decisions in order to keep us within a dwelling, from having moved so many times while simultaneously staying in place. I know that, because of this, I walk with both an inability to settle and an understanding of home as the overwhelming, all-encompassing emotion of arrival.

We choose to adhere to traditions of the past as our rate of change increases exponentially. We’re taught Monopoly in order to play the game of capitalism. We clothe our bodies with the market as if it is embedded within our DNA. We assume wealth is interchangeable with value. We accept fiction as fact. We find meaning in that which we orient ourselves to. For those of us born into a world disoriented, it is in our reorientation that greater truths emerge. The reorientation process is a hunt for meaning and quest for acceptance. To consider existence is to consider the inevitable opposite: not existing. The greatest fear and truth does not discriminate, nor do natural and chance occurrences that are forceful enough to take one’s existence away. We discriminate. We establish and reinforce structural systems that continue to marginalize, oppress, lessen, and shorten the existence of the others. Finding truth and value in one’s existence is a constant survey of place-based fitness. It requires guarding our own sacredly-private while searching for and fabricating a collection of liberating publics. The closeted Queer body is perceived to be in a constant surveillance state of most private and most public, where heightened are performances of harsh stoicism and supercharged eruption.

Consider the closet: a room, ubiquitously understood, embedded with spatial implications. To come out of the closet is to shed the first layer of a thickened skin. Delayering the Queer body is non-linear and lifelong; it is a constant shedding of skins at varying intervals dictated by a spectrum of climate conditions one must snake through on their quest for home. “Coming out” does not rid the closet of what’s inside, nor does it imply that layers once worn can’t be put back on. The Queer body is Bachelard’s casket; delayering (coming out) breaks the dialectics of inside and outside; the most private and most public become one.

Consider the house: a collection of rooms, one being a bedroom encompassing a closet. If the closet is acceptance within one’s skin, the bedroom is acceptance within one’s space, becoming the second most sacred layer. Now we have two layers that one must come out from, each bound by four walls, each with spatial implications, one within the other as a nesting doll.

Consider the home: a collection of rooms, one being a bedroom encompassing a closet. In leaving the bedroom, one may walk down the corridor, through the living room, and make a stop in the kitchen before they are able to freely exit; each additional volume within this navigational sequence, too, has its own spatial implications. To feel at home is to know the ease of navigating these layers as one; to have a house is to know the boundaries that define them.

Home is an association of place, an unbound, non-physical ideological condition of security that begins with finding comfort in one’s skin; for the Queer body, home is first, quite literally, where the heart is.

On How to Design for the Possibility of Home

  1. Be the casket. Make an unimpeachable case that foregrounds your being within a logical operation.
  2. Unlearn conventions of domesticity embedded in the patriarchal dwelling; consider alternative methods of residence that prioritize identities over formal precedent.
  3. Design one sacred room with the affect of the first time, every time.
  4. Layer private and public. Reconsider thresholds and interstitial spaces as opportunities to connect and Queer one’s spatial experience.
  5. Enable spatial autonomy by prescribing the unscripted, embedding an aesthetics of access.
  6. Consider the skin on your back. Elevate the invisible preconditions of the site; celebrate and orient towards every edge.

Selected references — Ahmed, Queer Phenomenology · Bachelard, The Poetics of Space · Colomina & Bloomer, Sexuality & Space · Foucault, Of Other Spaces · McHarg, Design with Nature · Pallasmaa, The Eyes of the Skin · Potvin, Bachelors of a Different Sort · Solnit, Wanderlust
