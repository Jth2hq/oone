Design Rationale
Surface · Corrugated Directionality
Corrugated steel facades and brushed hallways establish one rule: every mark runs in one direction only. On canvas this becomes horizontal scan lines, each at a fractionally different luminosity. The interference of many near-parallel lines at close spacing creates the sensation of physical material depth — texture without color.
TextureGeometricSolid
Light · The Single Specular Event
Chrome spheres and polished tubes each contain exactly one bright point — a specular highlight that is neither ambient nor diffuse, but precise. The canvas borrows this as a single, slowly traveling brightness streak that sweeps the scan-line field. Life without color. Movement without ephemerality.
ExperientialGeometric
Geometry · Vanishing Corridor as Grid Logic
The receding corridor of lights — rows converging toward a vanishing point — is the strictest geometric image in the five-material set. It informs how the OONE grid behaves: measured, parallel, deep. Metal introduces the most rigid structural logic of all materials. The grid is not decorative; it is load-bearing.
GeometricExperiential
OONE · Canvas + UI Application
Canvas: slow horizontal scan-line field with one traveling specular event. UI panels: chrome-matte — no hue, all contrast. The corrugated rhythm informs spacing modules. Borders glow from a single directional light source rather than casting ambient shadows.

Design Rationale
Ephemerality · The Membrane Under Tension
The soap bubble is the project's primary ephemerality metaphor. Its surface exists purely in tension — color is not pigment but physics, a consequence of light bouncing between two surfaces 200nm apart. UI elements inheriting this quality should feel as if they could dissolve at any moment. Existence is conditional.
EphemeralExperiential
Texture · Grain as Membrane Skin
Photographic film grain applied over bubble surfaces simulates the photographic act of capturing something translucent. Applied at two blend modes — overlay + multiply — it becomes tactile: a surface you can almost feel. The grain is not decoration; it is the material's epidermis.
TexturePlayful
Behavior · Repulsion as Primary Interaction
Bubbles flee the cursor. Unlike most UI paradigms where elements attract toward the user's intent, Glycerin inverts this: the surface is shy, self-preserving. Repulsion — not hovering, not clicking, but displacing — is the behavioral fingerprint that distinguishes this material within the full system.
EphemeralPlayfulExperiential
OONE · Canvas + UI Application
Physics-based floating spheres with layered specular highlights and double film-grain overlay. Data entries appear as thermal patches in the bubble field. The lava-lamp navigation inherits this material's bubble physics. Mouse repulsion is the key distinguishing interaction in the system.

Design Rationale
Color · Transmission, Not Reflection
The pivotal distinction from Metal: polycarbonate does not reflect color — it transmits it. The mood board shows glowing colored panels, blue facades burning from within, orange and violet sheets where color IS the light passing through. This makes Polycarbonate the chromatic center of the system. Hue is structure here, not decoration.
ColorfulExperiential
Geometry · Ribbed Vertical System
Corrugated polycarbonate has strict vertical geometry — parallel ribs compressing and expanding as viewing angle changes. Both as architectural cladding and as wavy optical abstraction in the mood board, this pattern becomes the canvas: vertical bands of shifting width, color bleeding at edges, depth through repetition.
GeometricTexture
Blur · Ganzfeld at Everyday Scale
Unlike Turrell's room-scale ganzfeld, polycarbonate brings edge-dissolving color to architecture at human scale — a wall that blurs the world behind it. The thermal blur, the aura around objects, the purple blurred figure. Backdrop blur (CSS: blur 18px + saturate 1.3) is the specific property derived from this material observation.
EphemeralPlayful
OONE · Canvas + UI Application
Canvas: slow vertical color bands in screen blend mode. The most saturated theme — coral, violet, deep purple bloom. UI panels have backdrop blur active. Borders glow from within rather than casting shadows. Panels feel like apertures into a colored room, not surfaces in front of one.

Design Rationale
Grid · Plan as Palette
Le Corbusier's modulor is the strictest structure in the five-material system. The site plan seen from above — architecture readable as pure graphic design. The mood board confirms: construction drawings, Swiss grid typography, Chandigarh elevation grids. This is layout logic: measured, proportional, absolute. The grid is not decorative. It is load-bearing.
GeometricSolid
Color · Load-Bearing Polychromie
Le Corbusier's 63-color architectural system treats color as spatial force — red advances, blue recedes, ochre grounds. Not decoration: spatial operation. In OONE, the Concrete palette uses color only where it performs a structural function. A red element claims space. A blue element creates depth. Ochre is the neutral field they act within.
ColorfulExperiential
Form · Burle Marx and the Organic Resistance
Burle Marx's biomorphic forms — Copacabana mosaic, Ibirapuera lagoon — place free organic curves directly against the Corbusier grid. The resulting tension between strict module and fluid form is Concrete's visual character. Playfulness occurs only inside rigor. The free curve has meaning only against the grid that constrains it.
PlayfulGeometric
OONE · Canvas + UI Application
Only light-mode theme. Canvas: modulor grid at .4px, Corbusier rectangular masses with soft cast shadows, Burle Marx bezier biomorphs, béton aggregate grain at multiply blend. Color marks are deliberate and sparse — each red or blue element is a spatial claim, not a stylistic choice.

Design Rationale
Interference · Two Grids at 3° Offset
The moiré effect is not a surface treatment — it IS the structural mechanism. Two sets of flowing horizontal lines at a slight angular offset produce the interference pattern. The pattern is the result, not the process. The mood board shows this at multiple scales: wave-print textiles, topographic maps, the "Too Much Future" wave poster — interference as universal grammar.
TextureGeometricExperiential
Scale · Earthwork Compression
Smithson's Spiral Jetty and Heizer's Double Negative are drawings made at land scale — only readable from altitude. The moiré brings this to screen: each wave band is a contour interval, the canvas is a topographic map of a landscape in slow motion. Mouse position shifts the fault line between the two interference sets — the most direct material interaction in the system.
GeometricExperiential
Warmth · Grain as Material Memory
Wood grain is time made visible. The mood board shows plywood close-up, stain swatches in twelve warm tones, paneled interiors with a single blue accent note. The warmth of this palette — ochre, cream, brick — is memory embedded in the material itself. Wood is the default and home state of OONE precisely because it feels inhabited.
TextureSolid
OONE · Canvas + UI Application
Canvas renders two offset wave-line sets in slow interference. Mouse position shifts the wave fault — the most direct physical interaction in the system. Palette is the warmest; Wood is the default/home state. Grain is implicit in the wave pattern, not a separate overlay — the texture and the structure are the same operation.
